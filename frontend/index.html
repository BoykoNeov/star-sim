A close companion has stripped this star's hydrogen envelope near the end of its main sequence — the step a full two-star model would show, named but not drawn here — baring the hot, compact stripped core: a stripped star (Götberg 2018). This is the dominant (~70%) channel for stripped-envelope and Wolf–Rayet stars, unifying hot subdwarfs (low mass — a thin hydrogen envelope survives) and Wolf–Rayet stars (high mass — a bared, helium-rich core) as one sequence. It is one representative state (halfway through core-helium burning), not a time-track — so the age slider is disabled. Only the surface composition is measured (helium-rich at higher mass, hydrogen-rich at the low-mass subdwarf end — watch it flip with mass); the spectrum panel shows the star's real CMFGEN spectrum. A later chapter than the stripped-star story: here one of the two stars has already died, leaving a compact object (a black hole) still orbiting its living, hydrogen-rich companion — a real POSYDON CO-HMS_RLO track (Fragos 2023). Scrub system time: the living star swells until it fills its Roche lobe and streams gas onto the black hole — an X-ray binary — and the black hole's mass grows by accretion while the orbit widens. Only the living star is on the HR diagram: a black hole has no photosphere — no temperature, no luminosity point — so it's drawn as a schematic point-mass marker in the 3D and Roche panels, never a star. The released accretion power (in the caption under the time slider) is a standard L = η·Ṁ·c² estimate on the model's real mass-transfer rate — a mildly super-Eddington source — not a measured X-ray spectrum. The last chapter of a massive binary's life: the surviving companion has also been stripped to a bare, hot helium star, now orbiting the compact object its dead partner left behind — a real POSYDON CO-HeMS track (Fragos 2023). Scrub system time: the He star is the only one on the HR diagram (the compact object is a schematic point-mass marker — no photosphere). The composition panel shows its measured helium-rich surface (carbon- and oxygen-rich once the most massive ones burn on). The payoff is the endpoint: POSYDON predicts what the He star becomes when it collapses, and combined with the existing compact object that classifies the pair as a double compact object — a BH+BH / NS+BH / NS+NS gravitational-wave-merger progenitor (a LIGO/Virgo source), or honestly no merger when either ends a white dwarf (shown above the slider). This is POSYDON's own remnant prediction under one pinned core-collapse prescription — not a merger time (that needs the post-collapse orbit and a natal-kick model this grid doesn't serve). In the mass-transfer variant the He star overflows onto the compact object (a He-donor X-ray binary — the accretion cue fires in the caption); in the inspiral variant the orbit is detached and quietly shrinking toward merger.
